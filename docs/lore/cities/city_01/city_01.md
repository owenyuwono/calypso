## Overview

A prosperous market town of about 2,000 people, built around a natural spring at a crossroads of two trade routes. Not a fortress, not a capital, not a chosen place — just a village that grew because the water was good and the location was convenient. About 100 years old and still figuring itself out.

---

## Founding (Year 0-20)

### The Spring

The spring was not dramatic. Just clean water coming out of a rocky hillside where two dirt tracks crossed — one running east-west between the coastal towns and inland villages, the other running north-south between farming lowlands and highland pastures. A farmer noticed the spring because his ox stopped to drink. He told his wife. They built a house.

That farmer grew turnips and barley on the flat ground south of the spring. Within two years, three more farming families had settled nearby — all displaced by a bad landlord who'd raised rents. They knew each other. They trusted each other.

### The First Businesses

A tinker and his daughter showed up, traveling the crossroads selling repaired tools. He noticed farmers kept breaking plow blades and nobody nearby could fix them. He set up a forge — a lean-to with a pit furnace. That forge is still on the same plot, rebuilt three times.

A merchant came next. Not wealthy — a man with a single cart who bought grain from southbound travelers and sold to northbound ones. He realized the crossroads was the midpoint. He built a storehouse and stopped walking.

A retired soldier arrived in year three. Old, missing fingers, bad knee. Wanted nothing to do with fighting. But wolves kept taking livestock. He taught farmers how to set watch fires, build corrals, organize a defense. People called him the Warden.

### The First Wall

Walls didn't go up as a grand project. In year six, a wolf pack killed a dog in the settlement at night. The Warden organized work parties stacking fieldstone into a low wall — three feet high, enough that wolves wouldn't jump what they couldn't see over. Enclosed maybe forty by thirty meters. Each new family added a section.

### The First Crisis

Year twelve. Disease — livestock sickness, probably brought by a merchant's pack animals. Killed most cattle in weeks. Not many human deaths, but the economic shock was devastating. No draft animals meant no plowing.

The tinker's daughter (running the forge after her father died in year nine) organized a solution: tool-lending with other settlements, borrowed oxen from highland shepherds. It worked, barely. She became the settlement's first real leader — not by title, but by trust.

After the plague, the settlement started keeping a communal grain reserve. First formal institution. The merchant volunteered to manage it, was voted out within a year, replaced by the tinker's daughter.

## Growth (Year 20-70)

### From Village to Town

Population hit ~300 around year twenty-five. Informal governance stopped working. Disputes the Warden's successor couldn't settle. A traveling magistrate suggested applying for a town charter from the local baron — a minor lord who'd never visited and didn't care, so long as taxes were paid.

The charter cost a lump sum from the grain reserve. In exchange: legal right to hold markets, collect tolls, settle disputes through a town council.

First council: five seats — farming, craft, merchant, at-large, and baron-appointed (baron appointed nobody for fifteen years).

### The Wealthy Arrive

Around year thirty-five. Not aristocrats — successful merchants from larger towns, drawn by the market charter and toll revenue. They bought the best plots (south side, elevated, drier), hired local laborers at better wages, petitioned for permanent market stalls.

The tension was immediate. Old families built this place from nothing. Newcomers arrived with coin. This is still the core political divide, smoothed but alive. The noble quarter exists because wealthy families clustered on the south side. The park gardens between the two neighborhoods were originally no-man's-land — a neutral council member planted trees as a buffer.

### The Garrison

For thirty years, defense was volunteer farmers with spears. Worked against wolves. Failed against the first bandit attack (year thirty-two) — eight bandits robbed a caravan in sight of the walls. Took an hour to assemble twelve armed men.

The wealthy families pushed for a paid garrison, funded by toll revenue. First captain was a hired mercenary veteran who brought four men and recruited eight locals. Grew to thirty-five by year seventy.

### The Walls

The original fieldstone walls were extended and rebuilt over fifty years. In year fifty-five, the council authorized proper construction: uniform stone walls, four gates (east, west, north, south) with towers. Took six years. Most expensive thing the city had ever done. The wealthy families paid for most of it — and never let anyone forget.

## The City Today (Year ~100)

### Population & Mood

About 2,000 permanent residents. Tradespeople, shopkeepers, laborers, farmers. Cautiously optimistic. People feel the city is growing. Younger residents are more ambitious than older ones. There's energy here — new shops opening, new faces arriving.

### The Adventurer Economy

Over the last twenty years, young people started making a living hunting monsters and gathering resources in the fields. They buy equipment and consumables in the city, sell materials back. This is genuine, growing economic activity.

The council debates whether to formalize it (adventurer's guild, bounty boards, tax incentives) or wait and see. Some merchants love the new customers. Some residents find adventurers loud and reckless. The standard small-town argument about change.

### Politics

The council is still five seats. Current tensions:
- **Old vs. new families**: who built this city vs. who funded the walls
- **Growth vs. stability**: expand the walls or restrict immigration?
- **The at-large seat**: always contested, always dramatic. Currently fought between a baker (old family) and a cloth merchant (new family)

There's no mayor. Chair rotates yearly. Decisions need three of five votes. The system is messy but prevents any faction from taking over.

### Governance

Technically a chartered town under a barony. The baron is a minor aristocrat who's never visited. Receives annual taxes via courier. The city governs itself.

## Districts

**The Plaza** — The heart. The fountain marks the original spring, channeled through carved stone. Crafting stations here (forge, cooking fire, workbench) are descendants of the first emergency facilities. The notice board near the inn is the city's unofficial newspaper.

**The Market** — Grew along the trade road where travelers already walked. Originally blankets on the ground, now permanent stalls and shops. Loudest, most chaotic district. Merchants like it that way.

**The Residential Quarter** — Where the first families built homes. Narrow, organic streets following paths worn into the mud during the early years. Newer construction follows a grid. The old quarter is a maze navigated by landmark.

**The Noble Quarter** — Higher ground, south side. Pale limestone houses matching the walls. Arrived after the hard work was done. Quiet tension with old families. Better drainage, better views, mild resentment from below.

**The Park Gardens** — A flood-prone patch nobody could build on. Someone planted flowers. Now the city's quiet spot — gazebo, walking paths, a massive oak that predates the city.

**The Crafting District** — Separated from the plaza when smoke and noise got too bad. Near the garrison (smiths serve soldiers). Guild halls here are politically powerful. Apprenticeships are competitive.

**The Garrison** — Oldest standing structures. Occupies the area where the original palisade was thickest. Stone barracks were the first permanent buildings. Thirty-five soldiers, always underfunded, always overworked.

**The Gate District** — Inns and supply shops at the entry points. The main trade road enters east and exits west. Inns cater to travelers and adventurers.

## The Fields

The fields surrounding the city are mostly farmland, grassland, and scattered forest. Not particularly dangerous — just wild enough to have wildlife.

**Slimes** are the most common creature. A naturally occurring magical phenomenon — ambient energy condenses in moist, organic-rich soil, forming semi-animate blobs. They're like mushrooms or jellyfish. Not intelligent, not malicious, just territorial. Farmers treat them like pests. Adventurers harvest them for jelly (useful in crafting). Kill them all, new ones form within days.

**Wolves** live in the wooded areas. Real wolves — not magical, not cursed. They hunt rabbits, deer, and sometimes young slimes. They avoid humans during the day. A livestock problem more than a people problem. The retired soldier's wolf management techniques are still taught.

Other minor creatures: insects, small critters. Nothing that requires a hero. The fields are safe enough that farmers work them daily. Adventurers hunt here because the materials have value, not because the world is ending.

Harder, more dangerous zones exist elsewhere — other regions that experienced adventurers travel to. This city is where you start, not where the final battle happens.

## Economy

### What the city produces
Grain, vegetables, forged tools and weapons, processed monster materials (jelly, fur, bone), cooked food, preserved fish, simple crafted goods.

### What the city imports
Metal ore (beyond local deposits), cloth, spices, salt, luxury goods from the coast, timber for construction, specialized weapons from master smiths elsewhere.

### The adventurer loop
Adventurers go out → kill monsters, gather resources → bring materials back → sell to merchants or craft into gear → buy supplies → go out again. The city sits in the middle, buying low and selling high. The forge turns ore into weapons. The cooking fire turns fish into meals. Money flows in a circle.

### What's expensive
Good weapons, healing potions (jelly ingredient requires fighting slimes), imported goods.

### What's cheap
Basic food (sardines), raw materials (stone, branches), copper ore (oversupply).

## Daily Life

**Morning**: Gates open. Farmers head to fields. Bakery fires have been going since 4 AM. The forge starts. Market stalls set up. People fill water jugs at the fountain and gossip.

**Midday**: Market at peak. Traveling merchants arrive. Adventurers return with morning finds. The tavern serves lunch.

**Afternoon**: Market winds down. Crafters at work. Garrison drills. Children play in the park. Fishing at the river spots.

**Dusk**: Market closes. Tavern fills. Social life moves indoors. Dinner at home or the tavern.

**Night**: Tavern stays open late. Guards patrol walls. The forge banks its coals. Quiet but not silent.

### Food
Staple diet: bread, root vegetables, river fish, porridge. Meat is a luxury (chicken and pork). Fish stew is the city's signature dish — every household has their recipe and will argue about the right way to make it.

### Drinks
Local barley beer (not great, but cheap). Imported mead (popular with adventurers). Imported wine (expensive).

### Music
A fiddle player and a drummer at the tavern most nights. Folk tunes, work songs, the occasional ballad. The fiddle player is a farmer's son who won't admit he wants to be a musician.

### Holidays
- **Spring Market** (early spring): three-day extended market, biggest commercial event
- **Founder's Day** (late spring): commemorates the first house at the spring. Ceremony, communal meal, games
- **Harvest Feast** (autumn): the farming families' holiday, everyone eats too much
- **Vigil Night** (winter solstice): torches along the walls, candles in windows. Acknowledges the dark and the cold together.

## Small Details That Feel True

- The spring water tastes slightly mineral, slightly sweet. Newcomers comment. Locals don't notice.
- The forge smells like iron and charcoal. You can smell it from the plaza on the right wind.
- Cats everywhere. Showed up during the cattle plague. Keep rats from grain stores. Nobody owns them, everyone feeds them.
- Children play "wolf and sheep" — tag with roles, a chalk circle for the wall, one kid is the Warden.
- The ancient oak in the park predates the city. People treat it with quiet reverence.
- The walls are highest near the gates and lowest at the back where there's only farmland. The garrison captain considers this a problem.
- 140m × 100m walled area. You can walk gate to gate in five minutes. Everyone is one degree of separation from everyone else. Privacy is a luxury.
